Product Manager?

Chapter 6 Do you like the work?

Architect? didn't ask whether you were smart enough to be an architect. It asked whether you liked what architects actually do all day. The answer isn't found in the portfolio. It's found in whether you enjoy standing in the rain on a construction site arguing about a beam specification with a contractor who has been doing this for thirty years. Whether that argument — winning it, losing it, finding the compromise — is the kind of thing that energizes you or depletes you.

I studied architecture and urban planning — not as a stepping stone but as a genuine interest in how cities work and fail the people who live in them. That led me to run product development labs at Drexel University, to design products for the built environment, to write about how transit and television shape urban culture. It eventually led me to bikes: specifically to Social Bicycles in New York City, one of the first companies to put GPS-connected smart locks into city bike-share systems. From there the path branched and doubled back. I moonlighted for companies as different as an Uber competitor in India and an AI advertising startup, before moving to Udacity, then Google, then Meta. The arc from academic to civic to early-stage startup to scale-up to Fortune 10 took about twenty years, several industries, and more than one country. I didn't plan it. I followed the question at every fork — the same one that book was asking: not whether I was technically qualified for the next thing, but whether I'd like what the next thing actually required all day.

Same question here. Slightly different rain.

I've run versions of these exercises with more than a hundred people exploring PM roles — and had the career conversations with hundreds more who were deciding whether to enter PM, stay in it, or leave it for something better suited. The exercises aren't theoretical. They emerged from watching what actually separated people who found the job energizing from people who found it grinding, even when both groups were technically competent. The difference was almost never about capability. It was about fit.

You've seen the job described from the outside: the three questions, the traits, the mental models, the peer map, the tools. Now we stop describing. We test.

This chapter is six exercises. Each one simulates a real PM experience. Do them — not as hypotheticals, but as actual tasks you actually do. The feeling you have during and after each one is the data. Not the result. The feeling.

There is no score. "I hated this" is as useful as "I couldn't stop." Both are honest. Both tell you something the job description won't.

One note on what these exercises are not: they are not case interview practice. A case interview asks you to perform PM thinking for an audience that will evaluate your framework. These exercises ask you to do PM thinking for yourself and notice what the experience is like. The difference matters enormously. Many people who interview well hate the job. Many people who find PM interviews awkward would love the actual work. The performance and the experience are different things.

6.1 Exercise 1: The Inbox

The scenario:

It's Monday, 8:17am. You open Slack. Here is what's waiting:

[Slack, 11:43pm Sunday] From: Raj (CEO) — "Just saw Notion's new AI feature announcement. We need a response. Can you put something together by EOD tomorrow? Doesn't need to be long." He linked the TechCrunch article.

[Slack, 7:52am Monday] From: Daniela (Engineering Lead) — "Hey, quick heads up — the sprint planning doc has a conflict. The onboarding flow work and the search refactor both need the shared component library at the same time. Engineering says we have to pick one. Need your call before standup at 10."

[Slack, 8:04am Monday] From: Marcus (Customer Success) — "Flagging this as urgent: Enterprise client Meridian Health is asking why their exported reports don't include the new date filters. They're presenting to their board Thursday. Our CSM thinks they'll escalate if we don't have an answer today."

[Email, received 7:58am] From: Priya (VP Marketing) — "Quick ask — can we do a 30-min sync this week to align on Q3 messaging for the launch? My team starts writing copy on Wednesday and wants PM input first."

[Jira notification, 6:14am] — Three tickets moved to "blocked" overnight. One has a comment: "waiting for PM sign-off on the data model decision we discussed last Thursday."

[Slack, 8:11am] From: your manager — "Do you have 15 min before standup? Want to loop you in on some reorg conversations."

Your first meeting is standup at 10:00am. You have 103 minutes.

Do this: Write down, right now, what you do first. Not what a PM "should" do. What you would actually do, in what order, and why. Give yourself a real time budget: 103 minutes split across these items.

Then write down how you feel about the things you deprioritize. Not whether you're making the right call — how it feels to leave them unresolved while you focus elsewhere.

What you're actually testing: Your tolerance for the constantly replenishing queue of competing demands. The inbox is every morning. It does not simplify with experience. The items get higher stakes — the CEO's message involves more, the escalation comes from a bigger client — but the structure is the same. Something is always waiting. Something is always more urgent than you planned for.

A worked example: A PM with good inbox instincts reads the Daniela message first, not because it's most urgent in the abstract, but because it has a hard deadline (standup at 10) and an irreversible downstream effect (sprint planning locks the team's next two weeks). They send a quick reply: "Morning — let's talk at 9:45 before standup. My lean is toward the onboarding flow but I want to hear the full constraint first." Then they read the Meridian Health escalation and ping Marcus: "On this — do you know if the date filter issue is a bug or a known limitation? If it's a bug I'll escalate to eng right now. If it's a known gap I'll draft a response you can send." Then they look at the data model Jira tickets. This takes twelve minutes. The CEO's competitive analysis and the reorg conversation from their manager both get deferred — not because they're unimportant but because they don't have hard deadlines before 10am and a rushed response to either is worse than a thoughtful one later.

What to notice about yourself:

  • Did you feel energized by the triage — like sorting the priorities was a puzzle you wanted to solve?
  • Did you feel anxious about the things you left unresolved?
  • Did you find yourself wanting to handle everything before starting anything?
  • Did you notice a clear instinct about what mattered most, or did all six items feel equally urgent?

PMs who thrive in this job usually have a clear triage instinct. It's not infallible, but it's fast. They read the inbox and know within five minutes what order to address things in. That instinct is developed through experience but it starts as a disposition. If the inbox produced dread rather than clarity, that's important data.

In years of coaching PM entry and transitions, the inbox exercise sorts more reliably than almost any other. I've watched people who were intellectually sharp and genuinely motivated sit down with this scenario and feel immediate dread — not at the difficulty, but at the structure. The replenishing queue, the competing urgencies, the thing that will always be left unresolved at the end of the day. That dread is information, not a failure. I've also watched people who were supposedly "still exploring" the role and who lit up immediately — already thinking through triage before they'd finished reading. Those two reactions predict more than any interview answer I've seen.

Reflection questions:

  1. What did you deprioritize first, and was that the right call?
  2. How much time do you typically spend deciding what to do versus doing it?
  3. What would have to be true for the Meridian Health escalation to become your first priority?
  4. How did it feel to defer the manager's reorg conversation?

6.2 Exercise 2: The No

The scenario:

Your company's largest sales rep — the one who closes sixty percent of enterprise deals — has been pushing for a specific feature for three months. The feature would give enterprise admins the ability to export raw usage logs in a custom CSV format that their IT teams can ingest directly. You understand why he wants it: it removes a friction point from the procurement conversation and he can close deals faster.

You've done the analysis. The feature would take two weeks of engineering time. You have eight weeks left in the quarter. The two weeks would come from the onboarding improvements you've been planning, which your data shows affects the thirty-day retention rate for all tiers, including enterprise. You believe the retention work compounds more than the export feature, but you can't prove it definitively because you don't have long-run data on what the retention improvement would do to enterprise expansion revenue.

The sales rep's manager has escalated. The VP of Sales mentioned it to your VP at last week's leadership sync. You're not being asked to build it — yet. But the pressure is building.

Do this: Write the document that makes the case for not building it this quarter. One page. These components:

  • What's being asked for, described fairly — not a strawman version of the request.
  • Why the sales rep and enterprise customers legitimately want it (their actual motivation, not your interpretation of their bad judgment).
  • Why this quarter is the wrong time — not in general, but specifically, given these resources and these alternatives.
  • What you'd offer instead: a timeline, a workaround, a commitment to revisit with specific criteria.

Then consider: would you actually send this document? If not, why not — and what does that tell you?

What you're actually testing: Your relationship with saying no under organizational pressure. Writing the argument is the easy part. The hard part is the conversation that follows when the VP of Sales is in the room and your VP looks at you and asks you to defend the call. PMs write no more than they write yes. The ability to hold a reasoned position — not stubbornly, but with confidence and genuine openness to new information — is one of the most important and least taught PM skills.

In coaching people into PM roles, the gap between writing the no and delivering it is where most struggles actually live. Almost everyone who does the work can construct the argument — "retention compounds more than this export feature" isn't hard to see. The test is what happens when the VP of Sales is in the room and your VP is looking at you and the pressure is real. I've watched people who were confident and articulate in every coaching conversation buckle the first time the organizational pressure arrived. And I've watched people who seemed hesitant in practice become remarkably steady when the actual stakes were on the table. The written argument is practice. The room is the exam.

A worked example: A strong "no" document starts with genuine acknowledgment. "The request is reasonable. Enterprise admins have a real workflow problem — they're downloading PDFs and manually reformatting them for internal BI tools. The sales rep is right that this creates friction in procurement conversations with IT-heavy buyers." Then it makes the case precisely. "Building this in Q3 means deferring the activation flow work. Our thirty-day retention for cohorts who complete the new onboarding is 18% higher than for cohorts who don't. We have 4,000 users per month in the affected funnel. If we assume the retention lift continues at current rates, the compounding value of getting to that improvement now outweighs the cost of two weeks of deal friction — especially since we have three data points of enterprise deals closing without the export feature." Then it offers something real. "I'd like to commit to building this in Q4 if we see the activation improvement hold through September. And I'll talk to the sales rep about what the interim workaround could look like."

What to notice about yourself:

  • Did you find yourself enjoying the argument construction — the precision of making the case?
  • Did you soften the no into a "maybe" before you finished the document?
  • Did you feel the urge to add caveats that made your position more comfortable to hold?
  • How did it feel to imagine the VP of Sales reading this?

Reflection questions:

  1. What additional information would change your answer?
  2. What's the weakest part of the argument you wrote?
  3. How would you handle it if the VP of Sales overrode your decision? What would you do next?
  4. Is your "no" actually a "not yet"? What would have to be true for it to become a yes?

6.3 Exercise 3: The Room

The scenario:

You are in a design review. The meeting has eight people: you, three engineers, two designers, a data analyst, and a QA lead. You're reviewing the final mocks for a new search experience that's been in progress for two months.

The design is good. The engineers are nodding. But twenty minutes in, you notice something: the search experience is designed for the behavior you wanted users to have, not the behavior users actually have. Your research showed that sixty percent of users search with one to two words, never full phrases. The design optimizes for long-query search — it shows query suggestions, reformatting tips, boolean operators. None of those help the one-to-two-word majority.

You've been sitting on this observation for five minutes. The meeting is heading toward a go-ahead. Nobody else is raising it. The designers worked hard on this. The engineers are already mentally estimating the build. Your manager isn't in the room.

Do this: In your next meeting where something similar is true — where you have an observation that would disrupt the direction the room is moving — say it. Name what you see. Not to be provocative. Because the team needs someone to name it before they spend four weeks building the wrong thing.

After the meeting, write: What did you say? How did it land? And: how did you feel in the ten seconds before you said it?

If you can't find a meeting where this applies this week, write what you would have said in the design review scenario above — and then imagine yourself saying it out loud. What stops you?

What you're actually testing: Conflict navigation isn't about liking confrontation. It's about tolerating the discomfort of naming the real thing when it's easier to let the meeting end with ambiguity intact. The discomfort is real. The designers did work hard. The engineers are ready to build. Disrupting that momentum feels costly, even when it saves weeks of rework.

At Meta, a cultural principle helps reframe this: the stated hierarchy is Meta, Metamates, Me — company first, then your colleagues, then yourself, in roughly the spirit of a Navy crew. In the design review scenario, the PM who stays quiet is prioritizing their own comfort over their teammates, who will spend three weeks building the wrong thing, and over the company, which pays for the rework. Named that way, the calculus shifts. Naming the observation isn't confrontational — it's what a good metamate does. The principle doesn't remove the discomfort. It gives you a reason to speak anyway.

The PM's job in a room like this is to notice and name. "I want to flag something before we move to next steps. The design is strong, but I'm concerned it's optimizing for a user behavior our research suggests is a minority pattern. Sixty percent of our searches are one to two words. Can we spend ten minutes stress-testing the design against that use case before we commit?" That's the sentence. It's not an accusation. It's not a veto. It's an observation that the team needs before they lock in.

A worked example of not saying it: The meeting ends. Everyone leaves thinking the design is approved. The engineers build for three weeks. QA runs the test. The search experience works perfectly for power users and nobody else. Someone runs the search query data and notices the mismatch. There's a post-mortem. Nobody remembers who knew what or when. The three weeks can't be recovered.

What to notice about yourself:

  • Did saying it feel like relief — a weight lifted — or did it feel costly?
  • If you didn't say it: what specifically stopped you? Fear of the reaction? Uncertainty about whether you were right? Not knowing how to frame it?
  • Did the ten seconds before feel energizing (a puzzle to solve) or draining (a social risk to manage)?

Reflection questions:

  1. What's the worst realistic outcome of naming the observation in the meeting?
  2. What's the worst realistic outcome of not naming it?
  3. Is there a version of the observation that's easier to say — less confrontational, more curious — that still gets the information into the room?
  4. How often, in the last month, have you left a meeting knowing something important wasn't said?

6.4 Exercise 4: The Story

The scenario:

Explain your current project — or your most recent one, or the PM work you've done in a class or side project — to someone outside your field. A family member. A friend who works in a completely different industry. Someone with no obligation to care and no professional reason to nod along.

Two minutes. Out loud. Not in writing.

Constraints:

  • No jargon. No "we're building a platform." No "we're optimizing the funnel." No "AI-native."
  • Explain the problem before the solution.
  • Explain who has the problem before the problem.
  • No more than one number. (Which number would you choose?)

When you're done, write down: Did they ask a follow-up question? What was it? Or did they change the subject?

What you're actually testing: Narrative gravity. The trait from Chapter 2 that most determines whether a team will follow you through uncertainty. AI gives you unlimited words. It cannot give you the clarity that produces conviction, and it cannot give you the specific knowledge of your user — their actual life, their actual frustration — that makes a story feel true rather than constructed.

The two-minute oral explanation is harder than it looks because it forces precision. You cannot hide in a slide deck. You cannot rely on a chart. You have to know who the user is well enough to explain them to a stranger, and you have to believe the problem matters enough that the explanation carries some heat.

Why it matters for PMs: The oral explanation is not a party trick. It is the test of whether you've found the story that will hold a team together when the data is ambiguous or the roadmap is contested. Teams don't follow documents. They follow the story they can retell at 11pm when something breaks. If you can't explain your product to a non-specialist in two minutes, you haven't found that story yet — and neither has your team.

A worked example of finding versus not finding the story:

Not finding it: "We're building a B2B SaaS platform for small and medium enterprises to manage their customer data more efficiently using AI-powered automation workflows." The listener nods. They have no idea what you're talking about. They change the subject.

Finding it: "Imagine you run a landscaping business with eight employees. You have three hundred regular customers. Every spring you call them to schedule appointments. Right now that means your office manager spending two weeks in March just making phone calls and leaving voicemails. Half of them never get called back. We're building the thing that does that calling automatically — and schedules the appointment if the customer picks up." The listener asks: "Does it actually sound like a person?" You've got them.

The second version is specific. It's about a person (an office manager), a real problem (two weeks of phone calls), and a concrete outcome (scheduled appointments). It doesn't mention the tech. It doesn't mention the business model. It earns curiosity before it explains mechanics.

What to notice about yourself:

  • Did you find yourself reaching for jargon as shorthand?
  • Did the story get clearer or muddier as you told it?
  • Did you start with the problem or with the solution?
  • Did you feel proud of the explanation or embarrassed by how vague it sounded?

Reflection questions:

  1. What's the one-sentence version of who has the problem and why it matters?
  2. If you had to cut the explanation to thirty seconds, what would you keep?
  3. Which part of the explanation did you believe most? That's usually the story worth telling.
  4. Try it again tomorrow with a different angle. Which version got the follow-up question?

6.5 Exercise 5: The Call

The scenario:

There is a decision on your current project — or on a project you've observed — that has been in a holding pattern. More information is being gathered. Another stakeholder needs to weigh in. The conditions aren't quite right yet.

Before you do anything else: identify whether the delay is because you genuinely need more information, or because you're uncomfortable with uncertainty.

There's a test for this. Ask yourself: "If I had to decide in the next thirty minutes and there was no penalty for being wrong, what would I decide?" If you have an answer, the delay is probably about discomfort, not information. If you genuinely don't have an answer — if the missing information would actually change your decision — the delay is legitimate.

Do this: If the delay is about discomfort, make the decision today.

Before you make it, write down: What is the minimum information you actually need to make this call — not what you'd need to feel ready, but what you'd need to make a defensible decision? What is the cost of waiting another week versus deciding now?

After you make it: Write two sentences. Not whether it was right. How it felt.

What you're actually testing: Your relationship with uncertainty under time pressure. The PM role requires making consequential decisions at 60% confidence and executing as though it were 90%, while remaining genuinely willing to update when the evidence comes in. That tolerance is not a skill you acquire through frameworks. It is a disposition you either have or you're building toward.

At Meta, the delivery pressure is structural in a specific way: the dates on any roadmap are always closer than they appear. This isn't a management failure — it's baked into the culture. The velocity expectation is ambient, not imposed by any individual. Understanding this changes how you use the thirty-minute test. "I'll wait for more information before deciding" at Meta usually means "I'm going to miss the window." The discipline of deciding faster than feels comfortable is partly learned, and partly forced by working in an environment where the roadmap moves whether you've decided or not.

The hardest version of this exercise is a decision that affects other people's work. When you make the call, the engineer starts building in a direction. If you change your mind later, they rebuild. The cost of your uncertainty is paid in their time. This is the accountability that Chapter 5 describes. It doesn't get lighter with experience. Good PMs calibrate their confidence honestly and communicate the confidence level explicitly: "I'm making this call with the information I have. If the research comes back differently on Tuesday, I'll revisit."

A worked example: A PM has been sitting on a decision about whether to use a third-party payment provider or build a lightweight internal solution. Both options have tradeoffs. The third-party provider is faster but has a 2.8% transaction fee. The internal solution is slower to build but gives more control. The PM has been waiting for the CFO to weigh in for two weeks.

The thirty-minute test: "If I had to decide now, I'd pick the third-party provider. Speed is more important than margin at our stage, and we can renegotiate the fee when volume warrants it." The CFO isn't providing new information — they're providing cover. The PM makes the call, notifies the CFO as a courtesy (not a request for approval), and documents the reasoning.

What to notice about yourself:

  • Was the delay about information or comfort?
  • How did it feel to make the call without being sure?
  • Did you feel relief after deciding, or did the anxiety continue?
  • Did you want to immediately tell someone else what you decided — to share the accountability?

Reflection questions:

  1. What would change your mind on this decision after the fact?
  2. What's the specific trigger that would cause you to reverse it?
  3. How quickly can you make decisions when you're alone with no one to consult?
  4. Do you find ambiguity uncomfortable in a productive way (motivating) or an unproductive way (paralyzing)?

6.6 Exercise 6: The Week

The scenario:

You are going to look at a PM's actual calendar. Not an idealized version. A real one from a real working PM. If you know a PM, ask them to share one week — not their best week, not their worst week, a representative week. If you don't know one, post in a PM community on LinkedIn or Slack. Most working PMs will share this. They're often curious themselves about whether their week is normal.

If you absolutely cannot get a real calendar, here is a realistic approximation for a mid-level PM at a scale-up company:

  • Monday: 9am team standup (30 min). 10am backlog grooming with engineering (60 min). 12pm lunch, read customer support tickets (30 min). 2pm research sync with UX researcher (45 min). 3pm writing time — spec for upcoming feature (90 min). 5pm manager 1:1 (30 min).
  • Tuesday: 9am standup (30 min). 10am cross-functional sync with marketing and sales (60 min). 12pm async — Slack responses, email, Jira updates (90 min). 2pm executive review prep (60 min). 4pm nothing. 5pm nothing.
  • Wednesday: 9am standup (30 min). 10am design review (60 min). 11am data review with analyst (45 min). 12pm lunch. 1pm executive review (60 min). 3pm incident post-mortem (unexpected, 90 min). 5pm writing — follow-up on post-mortem action items.
  • Thursday: 9am standup (30 min). 10am three 30-minute stakeholder conversations (vendor, sales rep, support lead). 12pm lunch. 1pm user interview (60 min). 3pm nothing. 4pm planning next sprint (60 min). 5pm writing — roadmap update.
  • Friday: 9am standup (30 min). 10-12pm protected writing / thinking time. 12pm lunch. 1pm team retro (60 min). 2-4pm nothing (used for catch-up, often consumed by Slack). 4pm weekly metrics review.

Do this: Block your own calendar to match this week. Actually live that week — or as close as you can get with whatever access you have to a workplace or project context. At the end of the week, write three sentences: What energized you? What drained you? What did you miss?

What you're actually testing: The calendar is the job. The docs, the frameworks, the strategic narratives — those are produced in the margins between the calendar items, often late, often under time pressure from the next calendar item. Many people who like the idea of being a PM don't like the actual structure of the week. That's not a failure of imagination — it's a real incompatibility worth discovering before you've committed to the path.

Notice the ratio of reactive to proactive time. Most of Wednesday was reactive — the post-mortem happened to you. Tuesday's executive review prep was reactive to a meeting you didn't schedule. Thursday's stakeholder conversations were largely inbound. The proactive time — the Friday morning block, the Thursday writing session — is the time you protect, and it is constantly at risk of being consumed. If reactive time drains you, the PM calendar will drain you. If it energizes you — if you find the problem-solving of unexpected incidents more interesting than the planning you had scheduled — you may be better suited to this job than you thought.

The question of seniority: The calendar shifts with seniority. As a junior PM, more of your time is in execution mode — filling in the details of decisions that have been made above you, running the sprint, maintaining the backlog. As a senior PM or director, more of your time is in influence mode — the cross-functional sync on Tuesday, the executive review on Wednesday, the vendor conversation on Thursday. More of the day is spent in conversations that shape direction and fewer are spent in meetings that maintain operations. For some people, the junior PM calendar is more satisfying. For others, the senior PM calendar is. Both are legitimate. The question is which version you'd look forward to more.

What the calendar also doesn't capture is how different "the PM week" looks across company types and industries. At Social Bicycles — a bike-share startup of a few dozen people — a representative week included field visits to hardware depots, calls with city transportation departments, and whiteboard sessions debugging firmware behavior in operations across multiple cities. At Udacity, where the product was online education, user research meant sitting with a learner in a coffee shop for ninety minutes. At Google, the underlying operating model shapes everything: Google behaves like slime mold — expanding wide across potential resource areas, building aggressively where genuine traction appears, contracting quickly where it doesn't. A PM week at Google is partly about signaling and sensing. Every cross-functional sync is also a read on whether your product area is finding the rich resource that justifies continued investment. At Meta, the week is organized around velocity — the roadmap dates are always closer than they appear, the calendar is dense, and protected thinking time requires active defense against an ambient pressure to move. These are all PM roles. The week that came with each one was entirely different. Your reaction to the calendar above — too many meetings, not enough structure, the right amount of ambiguity — will tell you something real about which version you'd actually want.

What to notice about yourself:

  • Which day felt the most like the work you want to do?
  • Which meeting would you have skipped if you could?
  • When during the week did you feel most effective?
  • When did you feel most frustrated with the structure?

Reflection questions:

  1. What's the ratio of meeting time to thinking time in the sample week? Is that ratio comfortable for you?
  2. What would you add to the week that isn't there?
  3. What would you remove?
  4. At a startup, this week would be roughly the same but more compressed and more reactive. At a mega-corp, there would be more meetings and less protected time. Which direction would you rather go?

6.7 What to do with the results

There is no formula. Six exercises. Some felt right. Some didn't. Here is how to think about the pattern.

After guiding more than a hundred people through versions of these exercises — in formal coaching, in career conversations, through mentorship programs — I've seen the pattern clearly enough to say: the exercises rarely lie, even when the person doing them wishes they would. The person who spends an extra twenty minutes on the inbox exercise because they couldn't stop optimizing the triage is almost always the person who will find the job sustainable. The person who finishes quickly and wants to know what the right answer was usually belongs in a role where the answers are more defined. Both are legitimate outcomes. The exercises are not designed to produce a particular result. They're designed to surface a real one.

I've also watched the other side. Hundreds of people — talented, experienced, accomplished in adjacent fields — have decided through these kinds of conversations that PM was not the right fit. Some realized it quickly. Others had been working as PMs for years before a conversation clarified that what they actually loved was the strategy work, or the user research, or the collaboration with engineering — not the full accountability structure the role requires. Deciding PM isn't for you is not failure. It's clarity arriving at the right time. This chapter is for anyone who wants that clarity before committing to the path, or before spending another year in a role that doesn't fit.

If four or more felt energizing — if most of the exercises produced the feeling of "yes, this is the kind of problem I want to spend my time on" — keep reading. Chapter 7 is about how to get in. Don't overthink the ones that were harder. Every PM finds some parts of the job draining. The question is whether the overall shape of the work is one you want.

If three or more produced dread or flatness — if the exercises revealed a disposition better suited to adjacent work — also keep reading. Chapter 7 covers the paths out too. Architect? didn't tell people who concluded architecture wasn't for them that they'd made the wrong call. It gave them a map of where else their instincts might fit. A person who loved the story exercise but found the inbox and the no draining might be a better fit for strategy consulting, content design, or market research — work that involves narrative and user understanding without the daily operational drag. These are not lesser paths. They're more honest ones.

If you're split — two or three in each direction — that's the most honest answer of all. The job has hard parts and good parts, and how you weight them depends on the context. The person who found the inbox manageable but hated the no might do fine in a product context with a strong Head of Product who owns the hard stakeholder work. The person who loved the story and the room but hated the call might thrive in a PM role that's closer to strategic planning than day-to-day execution. Chapter 7 can help you find the context where the ratio works.

One pattern worth naming: If you found the exercises themselves more interesting than whether you "passed" them — if you spent time thinking about the design review scenario or the inbox triage even after finishing the exercise — that's a meaningful signal. The PM who finds the problems genuinely interesting, not just as a career path but as a set of puzzles, is the one who is most likely to be good at it. Engagement with the problems of the job is not sufficient on its own. But the absence of it is a warning sign that frameworks and credentials won't fix.

The self-diagnostic isn't a test. It's calibration. The job is the exercise. Pick the one that stuck.
