Product Manager?

Chapter 3 How PMs think

Engineers think in systems. Designers think in users. PMs think in tradeoffs.

That's the short version. The longer version is that PMs think in tradeoffs across multiple time horizons, with incomplete information, in conditions of political pressure, while managing the expectations of people who have competing priorities and only partial visibility into what you know.

Chapter 2 was about what you bring to the work — the dispositions, the orientations, the tolerances. This chapter is about how those dispositions become operational. The specific mental moves. The ways of structuring a decision in the moment that distinguish a PM who is thinking clearly from a PM who is thrashing.

I want to be honest about something before we start: these mental moves are not a methodology. There is no PM-think framework you can install and run. The moves described in this chapter are habits of thought that take years to develop and that manifest differently in different contexts and different people. What I'm giving you is a vocabulary for patterns that you'll need to develop through practice. The vocabulary is useful. Don't mistake it for the thing.

AI made generating options cheap. It made choosing harder — you're now picking from a bigger list, with less time to evaluate each one, while the AI confidently argues for whatever direction you're already leaning.

The judgment skills that define PM work were always there. They're just more visible now. There used to be friction that forced careful thinking: you had to do the analysis yourself, make your case with limited tools, sit with uncertainty without anything to consult. That friction is gone. What's left is the thinking itself.

3.1 Problem first, not solution

Bad PM: "We should add an AI assistant."

PM: "Support tickets about onboarding are up 30% month-over-month. Half are 'where do I start?' What's the smallest thing we can test to see if better guidance cuts that in half?"

This principle — problem first, not solution — has been a PM mantra for twenty years. It's never been harder to follow than it is right now.

The reason it's never been harder: in 2026, the solution often arrives before the problem has been properly diagnosed, and it arrives pre-packaged with capabilities that make it feel inevitable. Everyone on your team has been using AI tools. They've seen what they can do. The AI assistant idea comes into the room already half-formed — someone has mocked it up, or seen a competitor ship it, or read about it, or built a prototype over the weekend. The idea is concrete and the problem is abstract. Concrete beats abstract in meetings. The solution wins before the problem has a fair hearing.

This is the structural pressure you're operating against. It's not that your team is lazy or that they don't understand PM fundamentals. It's that the product development environment in 2026 generates compelling solutions at a rate that outpaces the discovery of the problems they're supposed to solve. Features are being built in response to what's possible, not in response to what's needed. This is the core failure mode of the AI product moment, and it produces impressive, polished, technically sophisticated products that don't earn adoption or retention because they solved a problem nobody had.

What "problem first" actually looks like when the solution is an AI feature everyone wants to build

Let me walk through what this discipline looks like in a real 2026 scenario, because the generic version of "write the problem statement before you think about the solution" has always been true but is being comprehensively ignored.

Your team wants to build an AI writing assistant into your product. The product is a project management tool used by small creative agencies. The team's reasoning: everyone is using AI writing tools separately. Why not integrate it? Users can prompt the AI in context. It reduces context switching. It's a competitive table-stakes feature.

Pause here. Before you write a single requirement, before you look at a single competitor's implementation, before you ask engineering how long it would take — what is the problem?

Not "users don't have AI in our product." That's not a problem. That's an absence of a feature. What is the actual problem that the AI writing assistant would solve?

Maybe it's: "Users spend significant time writing status updates and project descriptions that follow predictable formats, and this reduces their time for the work they actually care about." That's a problem. It's specific, it's about a user pain, and it has a measurable consequence.

Or maybe the problem is: "Users lose context when they switch between our tool and their AI writing tools, which creates errors in project documentation." Also a problem. Different problem. Different solution implications.

Or maybe — and this is the uncomfortable one — the problem is that your product manager and the engineering lead both use AI writing tools personally and found them valuable, and the team is pattern-matching from their own experience to a user base that uses your product in a fundamentally different workflow. In which case there may not be a well-defined problem at all. The desire to build the feature is the problem, not a user need.

You cannot know which of these is true without talking to users. Not surveying users. Talking to them. Watching them work. Finding out what they actually do with writing in the context of project management and where the friction actually is.

Here is the test I use: I call it the two-sentence rule. State the problem you're working on — not the solution, not the approach, not the feature — in two sentences. The first sentence names the user and the specific pain. The second sentence names what that pain costs them.

Example of a bad problem statement: "Users need AI writing assistance in their project management workflow." This is not a problem statement. It's a solution in a problem's clothing. There's no specific pain and no cost.

Example of a better problem statement: "Creative project managers at small agencies spend approximately three hours per week writing status updates and meeting notes that contain highly predictable information. This time comes from their billable hours, which directly affects their agency's margins."

Now you have a problem. You can test whether the problem is real (talk to ten agencies, find out if the three hours figure is accurate and whether it comes from billable time). You can size the problem (how many agencies, what's the margin impact, is this actually a pain point or just an inefficiency they've accepted?). You can think about whether an AI writing assistant is actually the right solution to this specific problem, or whether there's a simpler solution — better templates, smarter defaults, auto-fill from structured data — that addresses the same pain more directly.

The discipline is this: write the problem statement first, and require it to pass the two-sentence test, before you let the team discuss solutions. This is simple and profoundly uncomfortable. Simple because it's a writing exercise, not a technical challenge. Uncomfortable because it forces the team to sit in uncertainty about whether there's a real problem to solve, which is a worse feeling than building something.

The 2026 version of this problem: when AI makes everything feel like a problem worth solving

There's a second-order effect that's worth naming. AI doesn't just generate solutions. It also generates plausible-sounding problem statements, user personas, and market analyses that can be produced rapidly and that look convincing without being grounded in actual observation.

I've watched PMs produce comprehensive problem statements — with user quotes, market sizing, competitive context — that were generated by AI with minimal user contact. The documents look like research. They're not. They're plausible extrapolations from patterns in training data, shaped by whatever framing the PM brought to the prompt. The user quotes are composites. The market sizing is from a secondary source with unknown methodology. The competitive context is accurate as of the model's last training date.

This is a version of the flattery problem I described in the judgment section: AI validates any problem statement you bring it. You can generate a compelling case for why the AI writing assistant is addressing a critical user pain in an underserved market in about forty minutes. Whether it's true requires talking to real users and being willing to hear an answer that doesn't confirm your hypothesis.

The protection against this: treat AI-generated research artifacts as hypotheses, not findings. A persona document produced with AI assistance is a starting point for research, not a substitute for it. Use it to sharpen the questions you'll take to users, not to answer those questions.

Problem first by context

Startup. At a startup, the problem is easy to identify and easy to misidentify simultaneously. Customers are close. They'll tell you what they want. They will not tell you what they need, and the two are often different. Early-stage startups frequently solve the problem customers describe rather than the problem customers have — because the described problem is verbal, explicit, and available in your sales calls, while the actual problem requires observation and inference. The discipline at a startup is the weekly ritual: what did we learn from real user behavior (not stated preference) this week, and does it change the problem statement?

Scale-up. At a scale-up, there are now many problems, all legitimately real, all backed by some combination of data and customer evidence, all competing for finite quarters of engineering capacity. "Problem first" becomes "which problem first" — and the answer is not whichever one your largest customer asked for loudest, and not whichever one appears in the most support tickets. Those are signals, but they're not the answer. The answer is the problem whose solution compounds: the one where solving it unlocks other problems, creates a platform for future solutions, or removes a constraint that's currently limiting multiple user journeys. At Udacity, the metric the company tracked was degree completions — full nanodegrees, start to finish. That was legible to the academic founders, to press, and to enterprise sales. But it obscured what a large share of learners were actually doing: enrolling for specific skills, specific projects, specific modules — not credentials. When my team built the Enterprise offering for organizations like AT&T, Turkcell, and Mazda, we found that their employees needed targeted skill development for specific roles, not degrees. The actual problem wasn't course quality. It was a metric mismatch: the company had defined success around a completion event that many learners weren't pursuing. That insight challenged the assumptions consumer PMs, marketing, and the C-suite had all built their strategies around — and surfaced downstream friction the metric had been hiding, like scholarships and alumni networks gated entirely behind full nanodegree completion, making partial learners invisible to employers. Finding that problem requires thinking clearly about the system, not just responding to the loudest signal.

Mega-corp. At a large organization, the problem has often already been defined by someone above you, in an OKR that was written to survive a planning process, not to describe reality. "Problem first" at this scale means having the courage to say: "This is the stated problem, here is what I believe the actual problem is, and here is why the gap matters." This is not comfortable. It looks like criticism of the planning process. It may imply that the OKR was wrong. Most PMs at large organizations have learned not to do this, which is why large organizations build a lot of things that address the stated problem while the actual problem remains unsolved. The PM who does this — who names the gap between the stated and actual problem with evidence, not complaint — is doing the hardest and most valuable work available at that stage.

3.2 Leverage over output

Shipping is not the goal. Shipping things that compound is.

This distinction is not new. But the way it plays out has changed significantly in 2026, and the failure mode has changed with it.

The old failure mode for output-oriented PMs: you shipped a lot of features, the product got complex, users got confused, and the velocity that looked like progress was actually accumulating technical and UX debt that slowed you down later. The PM who shipped a lot looked good on a quarterly basis and created problems at the annual horizon.

The new failure mode is different. Because AI has made prototyping, first drafts, and initial implementations faster, the volume of things that could be shipped has increased dramatically. A team of five engineers in 2026 can generate viable prototypes at a rate that a team of fifteen couldn't in 2021. This means the leverage question is no longer primarily about whether you're writing good enough specs to get five engineers to build the right thing. It's about which of the fifty viable things you could build are worth building, and which forty-six you should throw away before they become anything.

2015 leverage: Write a spec so five engineers build the right thing instead of building the wrong thing or nothing.

2026 leverage: Decide which three experiments, out of the twenty-five your team could build in a week, are worth running — and then kill the results of two of them before they become roadmap commitments.

Your job moved up one abstraction layer. You don't primarily manage execution. You decide which experiments are worth running, which results are worth building on, and which promising-looking things should be killed before they consume attention and planning slots that should go to something better. The model can generate experiments faster than you can think of them. Your edge is knowing which experiments answer a question worth asking.

What leverage looks like in practice

At Meta, managing ads growth at scale, the leverage question was always about what to build versus what to let the system optimize. A large-scale ads platform has hundreds of variables that can be tuned — bid strategies, creative optimization, audience targeting parameters, delivery algorithms. The PM who thinks about output asks: what new feature can we ship this quarter? The PM who thinks about leverage asks: what is the one change—to the auction signal, the objective function, the bidding incentive—that, if we get it right, shifts how advertisers bid, how users engage, and how the platform balances supply and demand, improving outcomes on both sides without requiring a new feature for each improvement?

That's a fundamentally different question. It requires understanding the system deeply enough to identify the leverage point — the place where a change creates positive feedback rather than just adding a new capability in isolation. Finding those leverage points is slow work. It requires understanding your data model, your user behavior, your technical architecture, and your organizational incentives simultaneously. There is no shortcut. But the payoff of getting it right is asymmetrically better than the payoff of getting ten smaller things right.

The test for whether you're working on leverage or output: if your team doubled in size tomorrow, would you ship twice as much useful product? If yes, you're managing output — the bottleneck is capacity, and capacity scales with headcount. If no — if the bottleneck is decisions, clarity, or the quality of your bets — you might be working on leverage. Leverage doesn't scale with headcount, because the constraint is not execution capacity. It's the quality of the questions you're asking and the choices you're making.

Leverage and AI: the new multiplication problem

AI has multiplied the leverage of good decisions and the damage of bad ones. This is the part most PM writing about AI misses.

When a PM makes a good strategic call — identifies the right problem, sequences the right bets, builds in the right direction — AI-assisted execution means that good call compounds faster. The team ships faster, learns faster, iterates faster. The compounding works in your favor.

When a PM makes a bad strategic call — identifies the wrong problem, builds in the wrong direction, optimizes for the wrong metric — AI-assisted execution means that bad call also compounds faster. You build a lot of the wrong thing very quickly. The technical debt accumulates faster. The users you've failed to serve feel the failure more acutely because the product has moved rapidly to a place that doesn't serve them. You've wasted more time and created more misdirection in the same period.

The implication: leverage thinking has never been more important, because the stakes of leverage decisions have gone up in both directions. The PM who is optimizing for the right thing in 2026 is dramatically more effective than the PM who was optimizing for the right thing in 2016. The PM who is optimizing for the wrong thing is dramatically more destructive.

Leverage by context

Startup. Leverage looks like killing things. You can generate more ideas, prototypes, and early-stage builds than you can evaluate properly. Every prototype that gets built takes resources — engineering time, attention, planning space — that could go to something better. The PM who kills bad ideas before they consume engineering time is creating leverage. The PM who says yes to everything because "we should test it" is destroying it. At a startup, the discipline of saying no to interesting things is the primary leverage activity. Almost everything is interesting. Almost nothing is the right thing right now. Learning to tell the difference and act on it is the job.

Scale-up. Leverage looks like infrastructure — the things that make future work faster and better rather than just completing current work. The prioritization framework your team uses without asking you. The metrics dashboard that answers the recurring question without a weekly data pull. The documentation template that removes the first two hours from every planning cycle. The standards that other teams adopt because they worked. These are leverage investments. They compound in ways that feature work doesn't. The PM who builds infrastructure for the team is often less visible than the PM who ships features — their work doesn't appear in launch announcements — but they create more value over time.

Mega-corp. Leverage looks like influence. The relationships that let you short-circuit a six-week review into a thirty-minute conversation with the right person. The platform investment that five teams can build on instead of building five parallel systems. The standard you establish that other teams adopt voluntarily because it's better than what they had. The PM at a mega-corp who understands where the organizational leverage points are — which decisions, relationships, and investments create asymmetric influence — can have an impact that looks disproportionate to their formal authority. The ones who don't understand leverage at that scale produce good work that has no organizational resonance.

3.3 Sequencing is strategy

Roadmaps are lies. Sequences are truth.

This needs unpacking, because the claim sounds more provocative than it is. Roadmaps are not lies because they're inaccurate about what will ship. They're lies because they imply a false precision about the future: that you know, with enough detail to put things in quarterly boxes, what you'll be building and why three or four quarters from now. You don't. The market will have moved. Your users will have surfaced a problem you hadn't seen. A competitor will have made a decision that changed the strategic landscape. A technical constraint will have resolved in a way that changes what's possible.

What you can know — and what actually constitutes strategy — is the order of learning. Which bets do you need to validate first, because later bets depend on them? Which assumptions carry the most risk, and how do you sequence your work to test them before you've committed resources to the things that depend on them?

This is the distinction between a roadmap and a sequence. A roadmap says: in Q1 we'll build auth, in Q2 we'll build sharing, in Q3 we'll build AI features. A sequence says: auth is the precondition for sharing; but we should build a minimal sharing feature before we design auth fully, because we don't know yet whether our users will share in a way that has security implications — and if they won't, our auth requirements simplify significantly. If sharing is adopted more than we expect, we learn something about our user's workflow that changes our read on whether the AI feature in Q3 is solving the right problem. The sequence is not a timeline. It's an order of learning.

AI can generate "Q1: Auth. Q2: Sharing. Q3: AI features." It cannot tell you that if Auth slips, Sharing is worthless, but that if Sharing ships early you learn whether the AI feature is even needed — or whether users care about sharing at all.

Sequencing decisions in 2026: the AI feature example

Here's a scenario that is playing out at companies across the industry right now, in 2026, and that illustrates why sequencing is strategy.

Your product team wants to ship an AI-powered feature — let's call it an AI recommendation engine that surfaces relevant content to users. The team has been discussing it for two quarters. Everyone agrees it would be valuable. The question is: should you build it now, or should something else come first?

The sequencing analysis looks like this:

What does the AI recommendation engine need to work? It needs enough user behavior data to train on, it needs the infrastructure to serve recommendations in real time, it needs a feedback loop so it can learn from user responses to its recommendations, and it needs a product surface where recommendations will be displayed and interacted with.

Now ask: do you have those things? If your user behavior data is thin — you have six months of data on a user base that's still growing — the recommendation quality will be poor at launch and will frustrate users who encounter it. If the feedback loop isn't built, the engine won't improve. If the product surface isn't designed to accommodate recommendations naturally, the feature will feel grafted on.

The sequence that looks obvious — build the AI feature because everyone agrees it's valuable — may be exactly backwards. The right sequence might be: first, invest in user behavior instrumentation so you have richer data. Second, ship a simpler version of the recommendation surface using manual curation, so you learn how users respond to recommendations in this context before you build the AI engine. Third, once you have data quality and a feedback loop designed, build the AI engine into a surface that you've already validated.

This sequence is slower. It doesn't produce an "AI feature" announcement in Q2. But it produces a better AI feature in Q4, built on data that makes it actually work, in a surface that users have already demonstrated they want recommendations in.

The PM who sequences well makes this argument clearly and holds it under pressure. The pressure will be real. There are competitive reasons to ship fast. There are organizational incentives to have an AI feature to announce. The sequencing argument requires you to say: the faster path is not the better path, for these specific reasons, and here is the order of learning that makes our eventual AI feature better than what we'd ship now.

How to think about sequence dependencies

There are two kinds of dependencies in a sequence, and conflating them is a common mistake.

Technical dependencies are structural — they constrain what's physically possible to build, regardless of what you know. At Superpedestrian, no firmware feature touching the motor controller could ship before the controller's base behavior was stable. Not policy. Physics. A firmware layer executing on a chip needs a stable foundation to interact with. You cannot meaningfully test a system whose foundations are still moving.

Learning dependencies are epistemic — they constrain what's worth building, based on what you've discovered or haven't yet. At JUMP, we could have built rider rebalancing incentives before we understood the demand asymmetry that was creating the distribution problem. The GPS infrastructure let us see where bikes ended up. What we needed to learn first was why. Bikes accumulated at transit hubs in the morning because riders were commuting to trains, not because of any failure in distribution logic. The real fix wasn't an incentive structure. It was a demand insight. A technically complete rebalancing feature built before that insight would have moved bikes in the wrong direction with great confidence.

That gap — building before you've learned what you need to know to build the right thing — is what most sequencing failures are actually about. The technical dependency is visible and legible. The learning dependency is invisible until you've violated it.

The napkin test: draw your roadmap. Cross out 50% of it. Does the remaining 50% still make sense as a plan? If yes, you had a sequence — the core logic holds even when things fall away. If no — if the remaining 50% is incoherent without the parts you removed — you had a list dressed as a roadmap. Lists fall apart when reality doesn't cooperate. Sequences survive.

Sequencing by context

Startup. Sequencing is survival. The wrong order of bets can burn runway on the wrong thing while the market moves. The right sequence is almost always: what is the cheapest test of the riskiest assumption? This sounds obvious and is routinely violated. Teams sequence based on what they're most excited to build, what's technically feasible, or what's most clearly articulated — not based on what's most risky and most in need of testing. At a startup, the riskiest assumption is almost always about users: do they have the problem you think they have, and will they use the thing you're building to address it? That assumption needs to be tested first, cheapest. Everything else waits.

Scale-up. Sequencing now has organizational weight. Teams have been staffed against the roadmap. Platform dependencies have been scheduled. Other teams are waiting on your deliverables. Sequence changes create ripples — they change other teams' plans, which affects their quarterly targets, which affects their managers' performance reviews, which produces resistance. The discipline is knowing which sequence changes are worth the ripple. Some are — when you've learned something that fundamentally changes what you should be building, the cost of continuing in the wrong direction is higher than the cost of causing organizational disruption. Some aren't — when the change is a marginal improvement, not a fundamental reorientation. Learning to tell the difference is a judgment call with real organizational consequences.

Mega-corp. Sequencing is political. What gets resourced first signals what the organization believes matters. The order of the roadmap is not just a tactical plan — it's a statement about strategic priority that will be read, interpreted, and responded to by people throughout the organization. Changing the sequence after planning is locked requires executive air cover or documented evidence of a bet going wrong. Both are available. Neither is free. The PM at a mega-corp who wants to change a sequence needs to understand this and build the case accordingly: not just "here's the better sequence" but "here's why the current sequence creates a specific, documented risk that justifies the organizational cost of changing it."

3.4 Calibrated conviction

You need a view. AI will validate any view you give it.

This is the most dangerous thing about working with LLMs in product work, and I want to dwell on it because it's less obvious than it sounds.

The problem is not that AI gives you wrong information. Most of what a good LLM tells you is true, or at least defensible. The problem is that it gives you confident, well-structured, internally consistent support for whatever position you walk in with. It is an extremely persuasive yes-and machine. You can prompt it with your hypothesis and get three pages of supporting evidence. You can then take that evidence into a meeting and present it as research. The research isn't fake — the individual facts are real. But the selection, framing, and emphasis have been shaped by your prior belief, amplified and polished by a model that was optimizing for a coherent, useful response, not for truth.

Before AI, building an airtight analytical case for a product decision required work. That work involved encountering disconfirming evidence. You'd interview users planning to make the case for feature A and one of them would tell you something that complicated your hypothesis. You'd pull data to support your position and find a metric that cut against it. The friction of research was epistemically useful — it forced you into contact with reality, including the parts of reality that were inconvenient.

Now you can build a compelling, cited, sophisticated case for almost any product decision in forty-five minutes. You can have your position confirmed, illustrated, and narratively structured without going outside of your existing beliefs. This is not a small change. It removes the mechanism by which most product thinking gets corrected.

What calibrated conviction means and how it's different from confidence

Calibrated conviction is not the same as confidence. Confidence is a feeling — a sense of certainty about a position. Calibrated conviction is an epistemically structured state: you have a strong view, you can defend it with specific evidence, and you have pre-committed to the evidence that would change your mind.

That last part is the critical element. Pre-committed. Before you ship. Before you've started to fall in love with the results. Before the team has been sold on the approach. Before you've written the launch announcement in your head.

Pre-committing to disconfirming evidence is hard in exactly the same way that the other traits in this book are hard: it requires you to take a position in public before you know whether you're right. "We believe this feature will increase activation rates by at least 15% in the first thirty days. If it doesn't hit 15%, we were wrong about the problem and will revisit." That statement makes a commitment that is professionally risky. If you're wrong, it's visible. Most PMs learn, fairly quickly, to avoid explicit pre-commitments in favor of language like "we expect this to improve activation" — language that sounds like conviction but doesn't create accountability.

Calibrated conviction requires you to resist that softening impulse. Not because accountability is comfortable, but because the alternative — shipping preferences dressed as experiments — produces no learning. You can't update your model of the world if you never specify what evidence would update it.

The pre-mortem as a calibration tool

The pre-mortem is the best practical tool I know for calibrated conviction. It works like this: before you make a significant product decision, imagine that it's six months from now and the decision turned out to be wrong. Badly wrong. Write down how that happened. Not "we shipped it and it didn't work" — be specific. What did users do? What didn't they do? What metric moved in the wrong direction? What did the data show that you would have seen earlier if you'd been looking for it?

I've used pre-mortems consistently since I ran products at Google's commerce platform, where bad calls at scale affected millions of merchants. The exercise doesn't prevent bad decisions. Nothing prevents bad decisions. What it does is surface the failure modes you know about but haven't wanted to examine — the assumptions you're making that you know are shaky but haven't named because naming them would complicate the case for the thing you want to do.

At Google, we were building features for small merchants who were trying to manage their product catalog at scale. There was pressure to ship a particular automation feature that would simplify catalog management. The case for it was strong: clear user pain, clean technical implementation, competitive feature gap. The pre-mortem I ran surfaced this: the merchants who would benefit most from automation were the ones who had the highest catalog complexity, and the merchants with the highest catalog complexity were also the most sensitive to errors — an automated action that produced incorrect results for a thousand products at once was dramatically worse than the same error made manually on one product. If the automation had an error rate of even 1%, it would be catastrophic for the merchants most likely to use it, and those were the merchants we most needed to succeed.

That concern was in the room before the pre-mortem. It just hadn't been named. The pre-mortem created a structure for naming it and taking it seriously, rather than nodding at it and proceeding.

Calibrated conviction and the AI confirmation problem in 2026

The AI confirmation problem makes pre-mortems more important, not less. Because you can build a supporting case so quickly, the mental discipline of explicitly imagining failure has to work harder. The pre-mortem can itself become AI-assisted — you can ask a model to generate failure scenarios for your product decision, and it will produce a list of plausible ones. This is actually useful. The model can generate failure modes you haven't thought of.

But there's a specific failure mode in AI-assisted pre-mortems: by default, the model tends to generate failure modes that are logically associated with the decision rather than failure modes that are politically difficult to name. It will tell you that the feature might have poor adoption, or that it might create technical debt, or that the timeline might slip. The failure mode that involves your CEO's personal attachment to the feature making it impossible to kill even when it's failing — that one tends not to surface unless you've specifically prompted for it in ways that feel uncomfortable to write.

The exception is companies that have built strong cultures of internal transparency — teams that write publicly, log decisions and debates, and commit executive perspective to mine-able internal records. At those companies, AI tools applied to that corpus can produce organizational calibration grounded in the actual voice of the people who hold decision authority, in ways that rival what you'd get from conversations alone. The organizational intelligence that a company like Meta generates through its internal writing culture — constant logging of decisions, executives on the record in forums, debates committed to searchable text — creates a kind of institutional memory that can actually be queried with rigor. The relevant gap at those companies isn't "is the political context available to the model" — it's "has the relevant context been committed to writing yet." There are always gaps: the conversation that didn't get logged, the latest perspective not yet on the record. But the degree of organizational calibration available through well-maintained internal corpora at transparent companies rivals what's achievable at companies like Google, where siloing is structural — Directors are often infighting precisely because context is not shared across organizations, a dynamic that appears intentional by design rather than accidental drift. At siloed organizations, where relevant intelligence lives in relationships and hallway conversations rather than records, the gap is large enough that no amount of prompting closes it.

Calibrated conviction in 2026: use AI to generate the logical failure modes. For organizational and political failure modes, the right method depends on what your company has committed to writing. At organizations that build transparently in public — where decisions, debates, and executive perspective are on the record and mine-able — AI applied to those records can provide meaningful organizational calibration. At more siloed companies, do that analysis yourself, in conversation with people who know the context. Either way: be specific about what evidence you'll be looking for. Write it down before you ship. Check it after.

How conviction needs to survive different environments

Startup. At a startup, conviction is a survival mechanism. Nobody will follow you into uncertainty if you don't project confidence. But uncalibrated conviction kills startups. The founders and PMs who survive the early stage are the ones who hold conviction loosely enough to update when the data comes in and firmly enough that the team doesn't lose faith during every weekly review. This sounds paradoxical. In practice it looks like: projecting confidence in the direction while maintaining genuine curiosity about whether the specific bets are right. "We're building the right thing for the right users, and I'm not sure yet whether this particular approach is correct — here's what we're watching." That's not weakness. That's intellectual honesty in the register of leadership.

Scale-up. At a scale-up, you now have enough data to be wrong with confidence. The models can rationalize. The dashboards can be selectively read. The cohort analyses can be sliced in ways that support any conclusion. This is the most dangerous moment for calibrated conviction — when you have sophisticated analytical tools, a team that's been sold on a direction, and a planning process that has committed resources. The discipline is running pre-mortems before you build the case, not after. The case will be compelling once you've built it. The question is whether the case is right.

Mega-corp. At a large organization, conviction has to survive a bureaucracy designed to sand it down. Legal review, policy review, executive review, regional review, partner review. By the time something ships, the original insight can be unrecognizable. The discipline is two-part: first, write down what you believe and why before the process starts. Second, at every stage of review, distinguish between changes that update the substance of the decision (valuable, incorporate them) and changes that dilute or obscure the decision without improving it (resist them, with evidence). The PM who can maintain the thread of a clear position through a complex approval process, incorporating genuine improvements while defending the core, is doing a specific and difficult kind of work that most PM training ignores. At companies with strong internal writing cultures, that thread is protected by the record itself — the original position is on file and can be compared against what came out the other end of the review. At more siloed companies, the PM has to maintain it manually, because nobody else will and there's no record to surface it.

3.5 Holding the long view

This one doesn't have a clean aphorism. It's less a mental move than an orientation that underlies all the others.

PM work is systematically biased toward the immediate. Sprint cycles, quarterly reviews, launch announcements, weekly standups — the tempo of product development creates an environment where the near-term is constantly visible and the long-term is perpetually being interrupted by it. The meeting you have tomorrow is about what ships next sprint. The metric you're watching is this week's activation rate. The feedback you're reading is from users who used the feature you shipped last month.

This is not a failure of process design. It's a rational response to reality — the near-term is where the leverage is on a short timescale, and it requires attention. The problem is that PMs who are fully absorbed by the near-term lose the long view, and without the long view, the near-term decisions don't compound. You're making individually reasonable calls that don't add up to a coherent direction.

Holding the long view is the practice of maintaining clarity about where you're trying to go over a one-to-three year horizon while making decisions in a weekly cycle. It requires you to answer, at any point, questions like: what's the state of this product in two years if the current trajectory holds? Is that a good state? If not, what's the decision you need to make in the next quarter that redirects the trajectory? What are you optimizing for now that will be hard to un-optimize for later?

The long view and AI in 2026

Teaching at Singularity University in 2012, I spent time with Stewart Brand and Paul Saffo — two of the most rigorous long-view thinkers working on technology futures. A framework Brand developed, pace layering, offers the most useful lens I've found for navigating the specific challenge that AI creates for product teams trying to hold a long view.

Pace layering describes how different systems change at radically different speeds. At the fastest layer: fashion and art. Below that: commerce. Below that: infrastructure. Then governance, culture, and nature — the slowest-moving layer of all. Brand's key insight is that the faster layers innovate and experiment; the slower layers stabilize and constrain. The interaction between them is what allows complex systems to be both adaptive and durable.

Applied to AI in 2026: frontier model capabilities — which models lead benchmarks this quarter, which agentic patterns feel novel today — are operating at the fashion layer. They will change. The commercial applications that AI enables — AI-enhanced workflows customers pay for, features that deliver measurable value for specific jobs to be done — are consolidating into the commerce layer. And below that, infrastructure-layer AI — reliable text processing, classification, embeddings, commodity APIs — is already stable enough to build on without fear of obsolescence.

The long-view challenge for a product team is not "will our AI capabilities be obsolete?" At the fashion layer, yes. The question is: which layer are you actually building on? A feature tightly coupled to a specific model's current output is a fashion-layer bet — it will require reconstruction as that layer evolves. A product positioned around a user problem that AI helps solve more effectively is operating closer to the commerce layer — more durable, because the problem persists even as the implementation changes underneath it. A product that treats AI as infrastructure — as a reliable, improving layer of capability, not a feature — is the most durable position of all.

Paul Saffo's contribution to this: map the cone of uncertainty rather than picking a single prediction point. Good forecasters define ranges. When you're making long-view product decisions with AI capabilities in the mix, the question isn't "what will AI be able to do in two years?" That generates a single-point prediction that will be wrong in both directions simultaneously — underestimating some capabilities, overestimating others. The better question is: which layer am I building on, and what's the appropriate planning horizon for that layer? Fashion-layer bets warrant short horizons and frequent rebuilds. Infrastructure-layer bets warrant longer horizons and more deliberate investment.

The kind of long-view thinking that survives a rapidly changing technological environment is not "in two years, we'll have feature X with capability Y." That level of specificity will be wrong. The kind that survives is: "in two years, our users' relationship to [core job to be done] will look like this — at the commerce layer, durably — and our product needs to be positioned here to serve it." At Meta, working on ads growth, the long-view question was never "what AI capabilities will be in our ads products." It was "what will the relationship between advertisers and their audiences look like in a world where AI mediates more of the interaction, and what does that mean for where value is created?" That question held even as the specific technological answers changed. It was commerce-layer thinking held against a fashion-layer environment.

Holding the long view by context

Startup. The long view at a startup is the vision. It's why you exist, what the world looks like if you succeed, why that world is worth building toward. It has to be specific enough to guide decisions and flexible enough to survive contact with reality. Startups that maintain a rigid long view when reality is saying something different fail by staying true to a vision that stopped being right. Startups that abandon the long view every time quarterly results are rough fail by never having compounding direction. The balance is a long view that describes outcomes for users and impact in the world, with flexibility about the specific approach to getting there.

Scale-up. The long view at a scale-up competes with quarterly planning. Every planning cycle produces short-term commitments that can, over time, crowd out the long-term direction. The PM who maintains the long view explicitly — who can say, in every planning cycle, "here's how this quarter's work moves us toward the two-year position" — is doing work that most planning processes don't reward but that creates the compounding direction that distinguishes great products from good ones.

Mega-corp. The long view at a large organization is often pre-defined — there's a product strategy, a multi-year vision, a stated direction from leadership. The PM's long-view work is not to define the direction but to maintain clarity about whether the current trajectory is actually headed there, and to surface when it isn't. The PM who can say "our current quarterly work is moving us in a direction that diverges from our stated three-year strategy, here is the specific way it diverges, and here is the decision we need to make" is doing work that's uncomfortable and necessary. Most PM work at mega-corps involves executing on the near-term without interrogating whether it's consistent with the long-term. The ones who interrogate it and act on what they find are the ones who build durable products.

3.6 Try this

Exercise 1: The problem audit.

Take the last three product decisions you were involved in — features prioritized, things deprioritized, approaches chosen. For each one, write the two-sentence problem statement: who has the problem, and what does the problem cost them.

Now check: was the decision made primarily in response to that problem, or primarily in response to something else — a competitor's move, a customer's request, an internal initiative, an interesting capability? There are no wrong answers here. The goal is to see clearly how often the decision was problem-first and how often it was something else. Most PMs, when they do this exercise honestly, find that a significant fraction of their decisions were not problem-first. That's the starting point for change, not a cause for embarrassment.

Exercise 2: The leverage map.

Pick one metric your team cares about. DAU, revenue, activation rate, anything with a number.

Use AI to generate ten ways to move it 5% in the next quarter.

Now kill nine of them. In writing. For each one, explain specifically why it's the wrong bet right now — not in general, but for your product, your users, your constraints. The one you kept: write the evidence that would prove it wrong within thirty days of shipping.

Now ask: is the one you kept a leverage bet? Does it create the conditions for the next bet, or does it stand alone? If it stands alone, is there a different bet that creates compounding?

If you couldn't kill nine, you don't have a strategy. You have a list. If you enjoyed killing nine, keep reading.

Exercise 3: The sequence test.

Draw your roadmap — or the next four things your team plans to build. Now, for each item, answer: what learning does this produce that affects the next item? If the answer is "none" for most items, your roadmap is a list, not a sequence. Find the learning dependencies. Reorder based on what needs to be true before what.

Pay specific attention to your AI feature, if you have one. What needs to be true — in terms of data quality, user behavior, infrastructure, and user mental models — for the AI feature to work? Is everything that needs to be true being built first? Or are you building the AI feature before its preconditions are met, because the timeline pressure to have AI in the product feels more urgent than the sequencing logic?

Exercise 4: The pre-mortem.

Pick the most significant product decision on your current plate. Before you make it or finalize it:

Write: it's eighteen months from now. This decision turned out to be badly wrong. Write three specific scenarios — not generic failure modes, but specific stories — about how it went wrong. Include the organizational dynamics, the user behaviors, and the metrics that told you it had failed.

Then: what evidence in the next sixty days would tell you you're on the wrong path? Name two specific, observable signals. If you can't name them, you haven't pre-committed to anything. If you can, write them down somewhere you'll actually check them.
