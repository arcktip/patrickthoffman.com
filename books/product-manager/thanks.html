Product Manager?

Acknowledgments

Every book is a collective act, even when only one name appears on the cover.

Nargis Sakhibova, my wife, made this one possible in every practical and meaningful sense. She held down the family while I disappeared into drafts, created space for me to write even when that meant less time together, and never once suggested I should stop. She did this while we were raising our son Mark and making sure our English Setter, Archie, got the attention and the long walks he needs to stay healthy and sane. There is no version of this book without her patience and generosity.

My father, Timothy Hoffman, is the reason I believe that how you think matters more than what you already know. He modeled that early and consistently. I didn't fully understand why until I was already ten years into a career built on that principle.

Cody A. Ray has been my sounding board for longer than either of us probably wants to admit. He is one of those rare people who will tell you when the idea is good and when it isn't, with equal care in both directions. Many of the sharper claims in this book took their shape after conversations with him — mostly during the startup and scale-up years, and at Google, when these observations were still being formed.

Over the years I've managed more PMs than fit on this page, and each of them taught me something about the craft. Two of them — Mycal Lopez and Jimmy Kang — earn a specific mention here because the relationship outlasted the job. They stayed engaged with the thinking that eventually became this book, pushed back when an argument was sloppy, and kept me honest. Mycal also gave the first draft an early read and told me what was working and what needed adjustment — the kind of steer that only comes from someone who cares more about the book being right than about your feelings. You learn more about the job by watching it done well by someone you're responsible for than by doing it yourself. I'm grateful for what they showed me, and for the conversations that didn't stop when the work did.

Two people changed how I thought about product work in ways I'm still unpacking. Gil Arbel, at Superpedestrian, trusted me with something hardware-shaped and hard, and gave me the room to figure out what I was doing — which is the only way you actually figure out what you're doing. Arjun Chittoor, at Google, helped me understand what scale actually changes about how you think, and — just as important — what it doesn't. He also offered a framing I've never stopped turning over: that the job is just a role on a stage we play, and that through it we learn who we are and refine ourselves as people — with impact that reaches far beyond the work we do for the companies that pay us. Both of them are the kind of manager I aspire to be.

The engineering managers and leads I worked alongside at Udacity — Dave Fayram, Art Gillespie, and Gregor Stocks — and my current engineering partners at Meta gave me my best education in what a genuine product-engineering partnership looks like. Chapter 4 is largely a description of what I observed working alongside them.

Finally, this book benefited from early readers and conversations I can't fully reconstruct. You know who you are. Thank you for reading the parts that weren't working and saying so. It's the hardest feedback to give and the most useful to receive.

— Patrick T. Hoffman
