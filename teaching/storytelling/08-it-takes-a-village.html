It Takes a Village: Scaling Your Stories

Patrick T. Hoffman 2026

Everything in this series so far has been about the story you tell — your framing, your listening, your message, your delivery. There is a ceiling on that model, and every PM who grows in influence eventually hits it, usually harder than they expected. A story that lives only in your voice reaches exactly as far as your voice carries and no further. It stops at the edge of the rooms you are personally standing in. The transition from a good product manager to a genuinely influential one is, in large part, the transition from telling stories to scaling them — building narratives that other people carry, repeat, and extend when you are nowhere near the room. This final piece is about that shift: how a story escapes its author and becomes the organization’s, and why that escape is the truest measure of a storyteller’s impact.

The arithmetic of the single voice

Do the arithmetic of influence for a PM who relies on personal narration. You can be in a finite number of meetings, write a finite number of documents, have a finite number of hallway conversations, and each one costs your presence and your energy. The moment you turn to the next thing, the story you were carrying begins to decay in the rooms you just left. Meanwhile an organization of any real size is making thousands of decisions a week, the overwhelming majority of them without you present. If your product’s narrative only exists when you are personally saying it, then in the vast majority of the moments that actually matter, your product is being narrated by no one — or, worse, by someone else, whose story is not yours and may not even be friendly to yours.

The goal is to build a narrative so clear, so portable, and so widely held that the organization tells it to itself in the meetings you will never attend, in the decisions you will never see, in the words of people who may not know you started it. A story that requires its author present is a story that has not yet scaled. It is a beam you are still standing under, holding up with your hands.

What makes a story portable

Compression. A story scales only if people can hold it and repeat it accurately, which means it has to be simple enough to survive being passed from mouth to mouth without degrading. The test is concrete and unforgiving. Can someone who heard your story once repeat its core to a third person and get it substantially right? If not, it will not scale, no matter how sophisticated it is — because sophistication that cannot survive transmission is not an asset held by the organization. It is a private possession held by you.

A handle. The most portable narratives crystallize around a short, sticky phrase that acts as a handle for the whole idea. “Working backwards.” “Mobile-first.” I have spent years getting mileage out of a phrase like “the Rule of Seven,” which unpacks, in the mind of anyone who has internalized it, into an entire argument about spans of control and decision latency that I do not have to re-deliver every time. Crafting the handle — the exact words that will carry your message when you are not there to carry it — is one of the highest-leverage acts available to a scaling storyteller.

Robustness to imperfect retelling. A scalable story still makes sense when told slightly wrong, by someone who gets the emphasis off. This favors narratives built on a genuinely sound core idea over ones that depend on precise framing to work at all. If your story only persuades when delivered exactly right, it is fragile, and it will break somewhere in the second or third hop.

The mechanics of scaling

Recruit and equip the carriers. A story scales through people, so the practical work is identifying the individuals whose voices reach where yours cannot — your engineering lead, your design partner, a respected staff engineer, an executive sponsor — and deliberately equipping them to tell it in their own words. Equipping is more than informing. It means handing them the narrative so clearly, and connected so well to what they care about, that they can carry it authentically into their own networks.

Give it away and let them make it theirs. A story that people are allowed to adapt and add to travels far better than one they are asked to recite verbatim. When a carrier reframes your narrative in their own language and attaches their own examples, they are not diluting it — they are investing in it, and people propagate what they feel ownership of and drop what they were merely handed. Control the through-line; release the words.

Repeat until it is ambient. Scaling is a function of repetition over time, not a single broadcast. A narrative becomes the organization’s only after it has been said enough times, by enough people, in enough contexts, that it settles into the ambient assumptions everyone shares — the point where people no longer remember it was ever an argument someone had to win, because it has become simply how things are understood.

Instrument it into artifacts and rituals. Durable narratives get built into the organization’s persistent structures — the documents people actually reference, the metrics on the dashboard, the recurring rituals of planning and review, the onboarding material a new hire reads in their first week. This is Conway’s Law working for you instead of against you. When the narrative is woven into the artifacts and rhythms that outlast any individual, it stops depending on anyone’s memory or presence at all. The story becomes infrastructure.

Scaling into the agentic era

There is a new wrinkle worth naming, because it sharpens everything above. As individual contributors turn into people who orchestrate AI agents, their output scales non-linearly while their need for strategic context stays exactly constant — an agent will execute enthusiastically in whatever direction it inferred, which means the cost of an unclear or unshared narrative is about to compound. If your human teams operate in narrative silos, your agents inherit the silos and act on fragments. The scaling storyteller’s job does not shrink in this world; it becomes load-bearing in a new way. The clearer and more portable your narrative, the more faithfully it propagates not only through the people but through the systems the people are now directing. Alignment was always the product. It is becoming the bottleneck.

How to know it scaled — and how to know it scaled wrong

A story has scaled when you hear your own narrative come back to you from someone you did not brief, in words you did not write, deployed correctly in a decision you were not part of — the skip-level who uses your framing in a review you are only observing, the engineer who cites the through-line to reject a scope addition without checking with you first.

But there is a failure mode that hides inside the success: a story can scale wrong. A compressed, portable narrative propagates whether or not it is still true, and the same properties that let a good story travel let a distortion travel just as far — a handle that was sharp last year hardening into a slogan that now blocks the thinking it was meant to carry. This is why the integrity discipline from the opening piece matters more at scale, not less: once a narrative is ambient, correcting it is enormously more expensive than authoring it was.

The generosity that makes it work

Scaling your stories requires giving them away. It means being genuinely comfortable with other people getting credit for ideas you originated, with the narrative traveling under someone else’s name, with your own fingerprints becoming invisible as the story turns into the organization’s common sense. The mark of the most influential storytellers is not that everyone knows the story came from them. It is that the story is everywhere and no one remembers it needed an author, because it has become simply what everyone believes.

This closes the loop back to where the series opened. Storytelling for a product manager was never about eloquence or personal performance. It was always about alignment — a shared understanding, held across many people, of what is true and what to do. The most complete expression of that goal is a narrative so thoroughly shared that it no longer needs its teller. The village is telling the story now. Your work, done well, has made itself invisible, and that invisibility is not the diminishment of your impact but its fullest form.
