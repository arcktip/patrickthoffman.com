Storytelling for Product Managers
Session 3 of 8 Strategy Structure

The Five Questions Every PM Needs to Answer

Patrick T. Hoffman 2026

Most of the narratives a product manager tells are the same narrative wearing different clothes. The vision doc, the roadmap review, the new-hire onboarding, the bet you are defending to a skip-level, the quarterly update that has to survive being forwarded — they are all the strategy story, told at different lengths to different rooms. Because that story recurs constantly, it is worth having a durable structure for it. Not a template to fill in; templates make people write to the form instead of to the truth. A set of questions. Questions do not tell you what to say. They tell you what you must be able to answer, and then let the answers determine the shape.

Here are the ones I keep coming back to. Five questions, plus a sixth I call a bonus and never actually skip. Answer them crisply and honestly and you have a strategy. Fail to answer one of them and no amount of polish will hide the hole — which is the real reason to run them. They are as much a diagnostic as a communication structure. They reveal whether you actually have a story to tell before an executive discovers, in front of everyone, that you do not.

  1. Where are we — the team, the product — now?
  2. How did we get here?
  3. Where are we going?
  4. Why are we going there, and why now?
  5. How will we get there?
  6. Bonus: How will we know when we have arrived?

1. Where are we now?

Every strategy story opens on an honest, shared account of the present. This is the stable world before the disruption, and getting it exactly right matters more than it looks like it should, because it is the ground the entire rest of the story stands on. A precise, unflinching description of the current state does two jobs at once. It establishes you as someone who sees reality clearly, which is the whole basis of your credibility, and it manufactures the common ground everything downstream depends on. If the audience disagrees with your account of where things are, nothing after it can land, because you are not even standing in the same place.

The discipline is specificity and honesty in equal measure. “We’re doing well but there’s room to grow” is not a location; it is fog with good PR. “Retention among power users is strong, but we lose sixty percent of new users in the first week, and that first-week cliff is now the binding constraint on everything else” is a location — one the audience can stand on with you.

2. How did we get here?

The second question supplies causation, and causation is what turns a snapshot into a story. Explaining how you arrived does real structural work: it makes the present feel comprehensible instead of arbitrary, it names the forces still in play, and handled well it defuses blame before blame can organize itself.

This is where advanced PMs separate from the pack. The past can be narrated as a chain of mistakes to apologize for, or as a sequence of reasonable decisions that made sense given what was known at the time and that produced knowledge you now carry forward. “We optimized for large accounts in our first three years, and that was the right call — it funded the company. The side effect is a product whose architecture assumes a big, sophisticated customer, which is exactly why self-serve onboarding is so hard now.” That is not an excuse. It is a causal account that makes the present legible and points at the future without indicting anyone in the room.

3. Where are we going?

The third question is the destination — the transformed world the strategy is aiming at. This is the vision, and its job is to be vivid, specific, and desirable enough that the audience actually wants to get there. A weak answer is abstract and safe: “become the market leader,” “delight our customers.” Those are not destinations. They are the phrases people reach for when they have not yet decided where they are going and are hoping no one notices. A strong answer paints a concrete future the audience can picture and evaluate: what will be true that is not true now, what the product will do, what the customer will experience, what the business will look like from the inside.

4. Why are we going there, and why now?

This is the question that separates a strategy from a plan, and it is the one that gets underweighted most often. It has two halves and both carry load. “Why there” is the argument that this destination is the right one. “Why now” is the argument for urgency — the reason this is the moment to move rather than a safer next year. Together they are the stakes, the “so what” that gives the audience a reason to act instead of nod and defer.

The “why now” half deserves special attention because it is where inertia goes to win. Organizations rarely fail to act because they disagree with a direction. They fail to act because there is always a reason to wait, and waiting is free until suddenly it is not. A compelling “why now” names the window that is open and closing: a shift in the market, a competitor’s move, a capability that just became viable, a customer pain that has crossed a threshold, a cost of inaction that is quietly compounding.

5. How will we get there?

The fifth question is the path — the sequence of moves connecting the present to the destination. This is where the story becomes actionable, and its job is to make the journey feel credible without drowning the audience in detail. The failure modes are symmetric. Too little and the strategy reads as hand-waving; too much and you bury the through-line under implementation minutiae that belongs in a different document for a different audience.

The art is choosing the altitude the audience and the moment actually require. For most strategy narratives that means the two or three big bets — the moves that, if they work, get you there — not the full project plan. Sequencing is itself a strategic claim. What you choose to do first reveals what you believe the binding constraint is, and making that belief explicit is part of the story, not a preamble to it.

Bonus: how will we know when we have arrived?

The sixth question is what separates a strategy narrative that inspires from one that is accountable. What does success actually look like, in numbers, and how will we know whether the strategy is working in time to adjust? Answering this well does something for your credibility that nothing else does: it demonstrates that you are willing to be held to account, that your strategy makes falsifiable claims about the future rather than aspirations that can never be shown to have failed. Naming the metric that would tell you the strategy is not working — and committing to it in advance, before you know which way it will break — is one of the most trust-building moves available to a PM.

Using the questions

These questions are fractal. They structure a five-year vision doc and a ninety-second standup update with equal force; only the altitude and the length of the answers change. They also work as a live diagnostic in the room. When someone pushes back on your strategy, their objection almost always maps to exactly one of the six, and locating which one tells you precisely where the disagreement lives.

A closing note on honesty: draft your answers privately, for yourself, before you build any narrative at all. Then pay close attention to which questions are hard to answer. The hard ones are not communication problems. They are strategy problems wearing a communication costume, and finding them alone at your desk is enormously cheaper than having them found for you, out loud, in the review.
