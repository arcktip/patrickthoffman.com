Self-Talk: Telling Yourself the Story

Patrick T. Hoffman 2026

Every piece in this series so far has been about the stories you tell other people. This one turns the instrument around, because there is an audience you narrate to far more often than any executive or engineer, whose interpretation shapes your judgment and your resilience more than any other’s, and whom you almost never think of as an audience at all: yourself. Before you tell the organization where the product is going, you tell yourself a story about what is happening, what it means, and what is possible. That internal narrative is not a private indulgence sitting off to the side of the real work. It is the substrate your decisions run on, and I have come to think it is the most under-examined skill in the entire craft.

The story is always running

Humans are relentlessly narrative about their own lives. We do not experience events as a neutral log. We experience them as a story with ourselves as the protagonist, continuously interpreting what happens in terms of what it means for who we are and where we are headed. The psychologist Dan McAdams calls this narrative identity — the internalized, evolving story of the self that gives a life unity and purpose. The point that matters for a PM is that this story is always running, whether or not you attend to it. Your only choice is whether you author it deliberately or let it get written by whatever happened most recently and whatever mood you woke up in.

Take one objective event through two internal narratives. A launch underperforms its target. Story one: “I misjudged this. Maybe I’m not as good at this as people think, and it’s only a matter of time before that becomes obvious to everyone.” Story two: “This launch underperformed, which is real and worth understanding. Launches that miss are how I learn what I was wrong about — this is information, and figuring out what it’s telling me is the actual work.” The event is identical. The two stories produce entirely different next actions, different emotional states, and different quality of judgment about what to do next.

Why this is a professional skill, not a self-help topic

It would be easy to file this under wellness and move on. That would miss why it belongs in a series about impact. Once AI absorbed the production work, what remains as the core of product management is judgment under uncertainty. The internal narrative is not adjacent to that work. It sets the conditions under which that work happens. It governs three things directly.

It governs the quality of your judgment under pressure. A narrative dominated by threat and self-doubt narrows attention, biases you toward the catastrophic reading of every ambiguous signal, and pushes you toward defensive rather than optimal decisions. A narrative that frames difficulty as workable keeps a wider aperture open — and a wide aperture is precisely what judgment under uncertainty requires.

It governs resilience and persistence. Albert Bandura’s research on self-efficacy — the belief in your own capacity to affect outcomes — established it as one of the strongest predictors of whether people persist through difficulty or quietly give up. Self-efficacy is not blind optimism. It is a well-founded belief, built from evidence, that you can act on your situation — and it is partly authored, not merely issued to you at birth.

It governs the story other people receive. The confidence, calm, and conviction that make a PM persuasive are not primarily performance techniques you can bolt on. They leak from the story you are actually telling yourself. You cannot reliably tell an organization a steady story about a hard situation while telling yourself a panicked one; the panic finds the seams and gets into the room anyway.

Authoring it well

The goal is emphatically not relentless positivity or talking yourself out of real problems. The goal is accurate and constructive interpretation: a story that is true to the facts and that keeps you capable of acting well on them.

Separate the event from the interpretation. Between what happens and how you feel about it sits an interpretation — a story — and the story is not the same object as the fact. “The launch missed target” is a fact. “I’m not cut out for this” is an interpretation, and usually a distorted one. Simply learning to see the seam between the two creates room to choose a better interpretation.

Interrogate the harsh story the way you’d interrogate a colleague’s claim. Advanced PMs are trained skeptics about assertions — they ask for evidence, weigh alternatives, distrust the too-clean conclusion. Turn that identical rigor on your own harshest internal claims. “It’s only a matter of time before they realize I’m not good enough” is a hypothesis, and a remarkably unfalsifiable, evidence-light one at that.

Author from agency. The most useful internal narratives preserve your sense of agency — they frame you as someone who can act on the situation rather than someone to whom things merely happen. “The roadmap got cut and there’s nothing I can do” is a story that ends your involvement. “The roadmap got cut; the question worth my energy is what the best available move is now” is a story that continues it.

Situate the setback in a longer arc. A single event feels total in the moment; a career does not consist of single events. I have shipped hardware that broke in the field, launched products that missed, and made calls under uncertainty that a later quarter proved wrong; every one of those felt total on the day and reads, from here, as one data point in a long practice of shipping. The launch that missed is a chapter, not the ending.

When the story needs another author

A necessary and honest note. Self-authored narrative is powerful, and it is also not a substitute for real support when the internal story turns persistently harsh, when it stops responding to evidence no matter how much evidence you bring it, or when it starts affecting your wellbeing beyond the ordinary hard weather of a hard job. The techniques above are for the ordinary hard weather. If the story has become genuinely punishing or immovable, that is not a failure of technique and it is not a thing to white-knuckle alone — it is a signal to talk to people you trust and, where it helps, a professional. Authoring your own narrative includes the wisdom to recognize when the story you are telling yourself needs another author in the room.

Teams have a self-talk too

A team runs an internal narrative the same way a person does. After a miss, a team quietly authors a collective story about what it means — “leadership doesn’t know what they want,” “we’re the team things get dumped on” — and that story sets the team’s aperture exactly the way an individual’s sets theirs. As the PM you are, whether you want the role or not, one of the authors of that collective narrative. What works is naming the difficulty honestly and then supplying the agency: this was genuinely hard, here specifically is what it taught us, and here is the move it makes available.

The first audience

You are a storyteller not only for your organization but for yourself, and the internal story comes first — chronologically and causally. It sets the quality of your judgment, the durability of your resilience, and the authenticity of the composure other people read off you. Before you can tell an organization a clear and grounded story about where the product is going, you have to be telling yourself one. Tend to your first audience, because everything downstream — every decision, every room, every narrative you will ever ask anyone else to believe — runs on the story you are quietly telling in your own head.
