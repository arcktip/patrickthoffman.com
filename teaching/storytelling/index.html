Practitioner Seminar

Storytelling for
Product Managers

Eight sessions on the craft of product narrative

Patrick T. Hoffman 2026 Eight sessions

For a product manager to succeed, storytelling is not optional. It is critical and load-bearing. Despite recent advances in AI streamlining much of a PM’s day-to-day production work, storytelling remains mostly untouched. This matters because storytelling was always the actual job: taking a pile of incomplete, contradictory information, collapsing it into a direction you are willing to defend, and then holding an organization aligned around that direction long enough to build something. That was and remains the most critical contribution a PM makes to any organization.

This seminar is about the craft of storytelling for product managers. Those who use it will leave with a more deliberate approach to a skill that forms the bedrock of career success — and that remains stubbornly resistant to delegation to AI.

My target audience is advanced product managers who want to be the model others learn from. You already ship impactful products by influencing effectively and you know how much communication matters. But you may be less aware about the structure and mechanics behind communication that actually works, or the places where the craft is genuinely hard and might hold back those you want to benefit from your model.

How the seminar is built

The first of eight sessions introduces a framework of five load-bearing elements — audience, intent, stakes, structure, and through-line — that sits underneath every product narrative that holds. Everything after it is either a deeper treatment of one element or a specialized application of the whole. You can read the eight in order, which is how they are designed to build, or you can drop into the one that maps to the problem in front of you. They are written to work on their own and to compound when used together.

A word on what this is not. It is not a bag of delivery tricks, and it is not a case for spin. The through-line of the whole seminar is that a story has integrity only when the interpretation it offers is one you actually believe and would defend under hostile scrutiny. The techniques here work — which is exactly why they are only safe in the hands of someone using them for something real. Credibility is the compound interest of storytelling, and this seminar is, in the end, about earning it.
