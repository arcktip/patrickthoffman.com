Storytelling for Product Managers
Session 2 of 8 Listening Audience

Listen First, Listen Deeply

Patrick T. Hoffman 2026

The word “storytelling” carries a bias baked into it: that the craft is fundamentally about output — about what you say and how well you say it. For a product manager that bias is not just wrong, it is backward. The quality of your narrative is capped by the quality of your listening, and the cap is hard. You cannot change a model of reality you have not bothered to read, and you cannot read one you have not listened to. The most influential PMs I have worked with were almost never the most talkative people in the room. They were the ones who had listened so precisely that when they finally spoke they seemed to be reading the room’s mind — because, functionally, they were. This piece is about listening as the foundational discipline of narrative, and about the specific, effortful practices that separate the PM who hears words from the one who understands what those words are actually about.

Listening is the audience research the framework demands

The framework piece named audience as the first structural member: whose model are you changing, and where does it currently sit. Listening is how you answer that with evidence instead of projection. The default failure of communicators at every level is identical and well documented — Chip and Dan Heath call it the curse of knowledge, and it is rooted in a robust cognitive bias. Once you know a thing, you cannot easily reconstruct not knowing it, so you model your audience as a slightly less-informed copy of yourself and explain accordingly. The cure is not to imagine harder. It is to go get real data about the specific humans in front of you, and the instrument for that is listening.

Here is the strange blind spot in our profession. No serious PM would ship a product without talking to users; we treat customer research as non-negotiable rigor. Then we turn around and build the internal narrative — the one aimed at our own director, our staff engineer, our finance partner, the skip-level who controls the headcount — entirely out of assumptions about what those people are worried about. We would never write a PRD on a hunch about user needs. We routinely build strategy narratives on a hunch about leadership’s fears. Apply the same discipline to your internal audiences that you apply to your users, and half your communication problems dissolve before you draft a sentence.

Ask who: the STS habit that transfers directly

My training in Science and Technology Studies left me with a reflex I cannot switch off, which is to ask who of everything: who designed this, who benefits, who bears the cost, who was not in the room when the decision got made. That reflex is the same instrument audience modeling requires, pointed inward at your own communication. When you prepare to move a decision, the naive question is “what will I say.” The STS question is “who actually has to be enrolled for this to move, what does each of them want, and who is not in the room but will kill it afterward if I ignore them.”

Bruno Latour’s actor-network theory is the useful frame here even though it was built to study science, not standups. Latour insisted that nothing moves through a system on the strength of its own correctness; it moves because a network of actors has been enrolled and aligned behind it. A true idea with no network dies. A PM’s real work, seen this way, is enrollment, and you cannot enroll an actor whose interests you have not surfaced. Listening is how you map the network before you try to move anything through it.

The three layers of what you are listening for

Deep listening means hearing three things at once, stacked in order of increasing difficulty and increasing value. Most people stop at the first layer and then wonder why their narratives keep missing.

Layer one: the content

The surface is the literal information — the words, the request, the stated position. “We need this by Q3.” “I’m worried about the migration.” “Marketing wants a bigger launch.” This layer is necessary and it is also where the least understanding lives, because a stated position is a compressed, lossy summary of something much larger underneath it. Take content at face value and you will dutifully build the exact feature nobody actually needed, having satisfied the request without ever understanding the want that generated it.

Layer two: the interests and the fears

Under every stated position is a set of interests — the goals, incentives, pressures, and fears that produced the position in the first place. This is the distinction at the heart of Getting to Yes: positions are what people say they want; interests are why they want it. The director who says “I’m worried about the migration” may actually be worried about the outage last year that made him look bad to his own VP, or about his team’s capacity, or about his credibility if he champions a thing that then slips. You cannot address a fear you have not surfaced, and people almost never state their fears directly — especially upward, especially in a group, especially the fears that are about status rather than substance. The PM who hears only layer one argues with positions. The PM who hears layer two speaks to interests, and speaking to someone’s real interest is the whole difference between being resisted and being welcomed.

Layer three: the frame and the identity

The deepest layer is how the person makes sense of the world at all — the frame through which they interpret everything, and the professional identity they are protecting and expressing through it. An engineering leader who identifies as the guardian of technical quality will hear “let’s move faster” as a threat to who he is, no matter how sound your business logic. A finance partner who frames the world in unit economics is not being obstructive when she pushes on your growth story; she is applying the frame that constitutes her competence, and asking her to drop it is asking her to be bad at her job. Listening at this layer means noticing the recurring metaphors people reach for, the criteria they keep returning to, the kind of evidence that satisfies them versus the kind that slides off. When you can articulate someone’s frame back to them better than they can, you have earned the standing to reframe.

The practices

Deep listening is not a personality trait you either have or lack. It is a set of practices, and like any practice it improves with deliberate attention.

Listen for the question behind the question. When a stakeholder asks something, do not answer immediately; ask yourself what would make a person ask that. “How confident are you in the timeline?” is rarely a request for a probability estimate. It is usually a fear about what happens if you are wrong, or a test of whether you are being straight with them. Answer the question behind the question and you address the real thing.

Ask, then go quiet. The most underused instrument in listening is silence. The two or three seconds after someone finishes a thought are where the most valuable information lives, because people cannot tolerate a vacuum and will fill it with what they were actually thinking. Most PMs, anxious to demonstrate command, rush to fill their own silence and talk directly over the disclosure they needed. Learn to sit in the pause.

Reflect to test the model, not to perform. Paraphrasing back what you heard is genuinely powerful and has one failure mode: doing it as a visible technique, which people detect instantly and resent. The point is not to prove you were listening. It is to test your model and hand the person a clean chance to correct it. “So the real concern is less the date and more whether we’ll have the capacity to support this once it ships — is that right?” invites a correction, and the correction is the gold. A note that matters: reflection should surface and clarify, never amplify. Echoing someone’s anxiety back at higher volume helps no one.

Listen across time, not just in the moment. Deep listening is longitudinal. The offhand comment in a hallway three weeks ago, the topic that visibly tightened someone’s jaw in a review, the priority that keeps reappearing in their weekly update — these accumulate into a model no single conversation could ever produce.

Listen to the room, not just the speaker. In groups the most important signals are nonverbal and distributed: who tenses when a topic surfaces, who trades a glance, who goes quiet, whose energy shifts. The person who objects out loud is rarely the only one holding the objection; he is just the one willing to spend the social capital to say it.

Listening is where narrative material comes from

There is a generative payoff here beyond avoiding misfires. Deep listening is the mine that great narratives are cut from. The vivid customer quote that makes a strategy suddenly land, the internal objection you preempt so cleanly that the skeptic becomes a sponsor, the reframe that makes a contentious call feel obvious in retrospect — none of these can be invented at your desk. They are harvested, from having listened. The best product storytellers are, first, the best product listeners, and the fluency that looks like improvisation in the moment is really the recall of things they took the trouble to hear.

A posture, not a phase

The instinct to treat listening as a phase — do discovery, then switch to delivery — is a mistake. Audiences shift, situations move, and the model you built last quarter is quietly going stale while you rely on it. Deep listening is a permanent operating posture that runs underneath everything you narrate. Put it concretely: before any communication that matters, the first question is not “what will I say” but “what do I actually know about the people I’m saying it to, and how recent is that knowledge.” If the honest answer is “not much” or “not lately,” the work is not to draft harder. It is to go listen. The speaking is the last five percent. The listening is the other ninety-five, and it decides whether the five percent lands at all.
