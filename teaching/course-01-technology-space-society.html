Course 01

Technology, Space & Society

A 15-week seminar for undergraduate and graduate students

STS Cities 15 Weeks UG + Graduate Seminar

How do technologies reshape the spaces we inhabit? This course asks that question across fifteen case studies — from the postwar television to ambient AI — using the analytical tools of Science and Technology Studies. Students leave able to apply STS frameworks to new cases, trace the spatial consequences of design decisions, and ask who benefits and who bears the cost of technological change.

Course Overview

The course's central question — How do technologies reshape the spaces we inhabit? — is written on the board in Week 1 and stays there for fifteen weeks. Each session returns to it through a different case: the television, the automobile, the telephone, the personal computer, the smartphone, platform urbanism, micromobility, ambient AI, and remote work. By Week 15, students should be able to look at any technology-space relationship and produce a rigorous account of what happened, why, and for whom.

Three Analytical Moves

The method draws on Science and Technology Studies (STS) and compresses into three moves that are applied across every case study:

  • Defamiliarization — making the background foreground; looking again at technologies so embedded they have become invisible.
  • Tracing consequences — following the ripple effects of design decisions outward from the room to the city to the policy regime.
  • Asking who — identifying who designed, who benefits, who bears the cost, and who was not in the room when the decision was made.

Logistics

Format15-week seminar, 75–90 min sessions
AudienceDual-listed: upper-division undergraduate + master's

Week-by-Week Schedule

Click any week to expand anchor readings. Full reading lists for both tracks are in the Reading List section below.

  • Wk 1
    Introduction: Technology Redefines Space
    What does it mean for a technology to 'redefine' a space? Course framing and methodology.
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Hoffman, Patrick T., Technology, Space & Society — Course Introduction (2026) — @arcktip framing essay
    Winner, Langdon, "Do Artifacts Have Politics?" Daedalus (1980) — foundational & accessible
    Petroski, Henry, The Evolution of Useful Things, Ch. 1 (1992) — how objects embed decisions
  • Wk 2
    Hearth → Television
    How TV migrated the center of domestic life, reshaped living room geometry, and altered family rituals.
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Spigel, Lynn, Make Room for TV, Introduction & Ch. 1 (1992) — the core text — essential
    Spigel, Lynn, "Installing the Television Set" (1988) — shorter article version
    Hayden, Dolores, The Grand Domestic Revolution, Ch. 1 (1981) — domestic space before television
  • Wk 3
    STS Frameworks
    Actor-Network Theory, domestication theory, and the social construction of technology.
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Latour, Bruno, We Have Never Been Modern, Ch. 1 (1993) — most approachable Latour entry point
    Silverstone, Roger & Hirsch, Eric, Consuming Technologies, Introduction (1992) — domestication theory primer
    Pinch, Trevor & Bijker, Wiebe, "The Social Construction of Facts and Artefacts," Social Studies of Science (1984) — SCOT framework
  • Wk 4
    The Automobile & the City
    How personal mobility technology remade urban morphology: zoning, suburbia, the parking minimum.
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Hayden, Dolores, Building Suburbia, Ch. 1 & 5 (2003) — accessible suburban history
    Kunstler, James Howard, The Geography of Nowhere, Ch. 4–5 (1993) — polemical but vivid
    Shoup, Donald, The High Cost of Free Parking, Ch. 1 (2005) — the parking minimum argument
  • Wk 5
    The Telephone & the Threshold
    Privacy, the front porch, the home office — how communication technology reshapes the public/private boundary.
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Fischer, Claude S., America Calling, Ch. 1 & 5 (1992) — social history of the telephone
    Goffman, Erving, The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life, Introduction (1959) — front/backstage framework
    Schwartz Cowan, Ruth, More Work for Mother, Introduction (1983) — labor perspective on household technology
  • Wk 6
    The PC & Domestic Labor
    The home computer, domestic space, and the blurring of work and rest.
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Turkle, Sherry, The Second Self, Introduction & Ch. 1 (1984) — how computers provoke self-reflection
    Wajcman, Judy, Feminism Confronts Technology, Ch. 4 (1991) — gendered dimensions of domestic tech
    Nilles, Jack, The Telecommunications-Transportation Tradeoff (1976) — the original telecommuting concept
  • Wk 7
    Midterm Presentations
    Students present research proposals with primary sources and a clear spatial thesis.
    Note
    No assigned reading — preparation week. Students present research proposals. Undergrad: 5 min + 5 min Q&A. Graduate: 8 min + 7 min Q&A.
  • Wk 8
    The Smartphone & Third Places
    Coffee shops as offices, airports as living rooms — how mobile tech displaced and virtualized the third place.
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Oldenburg, Ray, The Great Good Place, Introduction & Ch. 2 (1989) — coins 'third place' — essential
    Turkle, Sherry, Alone Together, Introduction (2011) — accessible and widely read
    Carr, Nicholas, The Shallows, Introduction & Ch. 1 (2010) — spatial metaphors of digital attention
  • Wk 9
    Platform Urbanism
    Airbnb, Uber, DoorDash: how platform products restructure neighborhood economies and spatial use.
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Srnicek, Nick, Platform Capitalism, Ch. 1–2 (2017) — concise — essential framing
    Wachsmuth, David & Weisler, Alexander, "Airbnb and the Rent Gap" (2018) — Environment and Planning A — empirical
    Davis, Mike, Planet of Slums, Introduction (2006) — housing scarcity as platform substrate
  • Wk 10
    Micromobility & Street Design
    Dockless bikes, scooters, and the contested politics of curb space. Practitioner perspective on urban mobility.
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Hoffman, Patrick T., Dockless: What We Learned Building the Bike That Became JUMP (forthcoming) — @arcktip — primary practitioner source
    NACTO, Urban Street Design Guide (selected chapters) (2013) — professional design standards
    Sheller, Mimi, Mobility Justice, Ch. 1 (2018) — mobility as a justice issue
  • Wk 11
    AI & the Intelligent Home
    Smart speakers, ambient computing, surveillance capitalism, and the erosion of domestic privacy.
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Zuboff, Shoshana, The Age of Surveillance Capitalism, Introduction (2019) — essential — start here
    Mattern, Shannon, "A City Is Not a Computer" (2017) — Places Journal — free, brief, widely cited
    Sadowski, Jathan, Too Smart, Introduction (2020) — MIT Press — smart home critique
  • Wk 12
    Remote Work & the Office
    Post-pandemic spatial reckoning: what happens to downtowns, transit systems, and home square footage?
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Sennett, Richard, Building and Dwelling, Ch. 1 (2018) — ethics of city and dwelling
    Saval, Nikil, Cubed: A Secret History of the Workplace, Introduction (2014) — office history from desk to cubicle
    Florida, Richard, The Rise of the Creative Class Revisited, Introduction (2012) — talent and urban geography
  • Wk 13
    Studio Critique I
    Draft paper workshop and peer critique. Written feedback required from each student on two peers' drafts.
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Graff, Gerald & Birkenstein, Cathy, They Say / I Say, Ch. 1–2 (2006) — academic argumentation primer
    Silvia, Paul, How to Write a Lot, Ch. 1 (2007) — writing as practice — brief and practical
  • Wk 14
    Designing the Response
    How might architects and planners proactively design for technological transition rather than react to it?
    Anchor Readings (Undergraduate)
    Brand, Stewart, How Buildings Learn, Introduction & Ch. 1 (1994) — buildings adapt to change over time — key text
    Duany, Plater-Zyberk & Speck, The Smart Growth Manual, Part I (2010) — applied design response
    Aureli, Pier Vittorio, The Possibility of an Absolute Architecture, Introduction (2011) — formal response to spatial crisis
  • Wk 15
    Final Presentations
    Public exhibition of final research papers and design responses. Invited guests from practice and academia.
    Note
    No assigned reading — final presentations week. Undergrad: 10 min + 5 min Q&A. Graduate: 15 min + 10 min Q&A.
